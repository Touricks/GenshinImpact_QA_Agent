Annotation Difficulty Classification

All 41 questions are classified into five tiers based on annotation feasibility for RL reward modeling, not raw content difficulty. Each tier reflects how reliably a reward signal can be generated.

APure Fact
Annotation
Exact match against a single entity/attribute. Clear, unambiguous correct answer.
RL Feasibility
Fully feasible. Reward signal is crisp — answer is either correct or not.
Examples
NK-01 (region status), NK-09 (Yeloya’s rank), NK-21 (Piramida city role), NK-22 (Lamp Bearers founding)
BConstrained Narrative
Annotation
Check whether key factual checkpoints are covered and correct. Can be decomposed into a boolean checklist.
RL Feasibility
Feasible. Decompose into checklist items; reward = fraction of items satisfied.
Examples
NK-12 (Three Moon Goddesses legend), NK-32 (Bitter Ravine incident), NK-36 (full Lamp Bearer timeline)
CConstrained Analysis
Annotation
Verify cited plot points are real + check whether the reasoning chain is internally consistent.
RL Feasibility
Partially feasible. Factual grounding is RL-trainable; reasoning coherence needs preference-based signal.
Examples
NK-37 (8th vs 11th Supreme Lord hypothesis), NK-38 (Professor’s deception analysis), NK-14 (Kuuvahki vs elements)
DCross-text Inference
Annotation
Requires judging “reasonable inference” vs “over-interpretation” across multiple source documents.
RL Feasibility
Difficult but possible via multi-dimensional rubric scoring (factual basis, inference quality, scope).
Examples
NK-39 (Fatui faction split), NK-41 (Sampo across all sources), NK-15 (Columbina’s full arc)
EReal-world Resonance
Annotation
Evaluate symbolic/thematic interpretation that bridges fictional narrative to universal human themes.
RL Feasibility
Hardest tier. Requires subjective literary judgment; best suited for preference ranking (RLHF) rather than binary reward.
Examples
NK-40 (Winter Grass proverb — sacrifice & legacy symbolism), NK-16 (“False Sky” metaphor)

Describe the historical legend of the "Three Moon Goddesses."
▶ Show Golden Answer
Teyvat once had three moons — the Constant Moon, the Frostmoon, and the Iridescent Moon — each governed by a goddess. Afterward she entrusted the Constant and Iridescent Moon Marrows to Rauma and Arlecchino, restored some Kuuvahki to nourish the land, partially lifted world restrictions on the Traveler, and found the Traveler's kin's ship hidden on the moon. Compare the impact of the "Nail of the Heavens" on Dragonspine with the Celestial strike on Hyperborea. What are the similarities and differences?
▶ Show Golden Answer
Similarities: Both are punitive strikes by Celestia (Heavenly Principles/Phanes) using Celestial Nails — the ultimate weapon — causing regional geographic and ecological upheaval; both resulted in extreme cold and civilizational collapse; both left explorable ancient ruins. Differences: 1. Cause: Dragonspine's Nail has an ambiguous origin (linked to old Mondstadt); Hyperborea's was explicitly punishment for "the first angels rebelling against Heavenly Principles" — giving creation authority to mortals, leading to the entire angel race being degraded into modern Seelies. 2. Compounding disaster: Hyperborea's destruction also overlapped with the War of Vengeance (Nibelung weaponizing the moons), inheriting both the Nail's damage and Ancient Moon Remains, while Dragonspine only had the Nail. 3. Cultural legacy: Dragonspine has only cryo-extreme environment and scattered ruins; Hyperborea's survivors (Frostmoon Scions) preserved a complete Moon Goddess faith system and unique Kuuvahki power. 4. Continuity: Dragonspine's civilization is effectively extinct; Hyperborea's descendants migrated to Nod-Krai guided by the Moon Goddess, maintaining millennia of faith and organizational structure (Chanters/High Priest system).

Combining "The Legend of Red Miller" and the truth of the "Great Treasure," analyze how Red Miller's "Boundless World" ideal was corrupted into the Treasure Hoarders' creed of greed.
▶ Show Golden Answer
Red Miller's ideal: Born in Nod-Krai, childhood friend of the first Lamp-Forger. His friend Solovi (a Snezhnayan noble) shared this vision. Spiritual amnesia: Successors inherited the behavior ("dig ruins, find treasure") but forgot the core spirit ("mutual aid, break barriers"). 3. Organizational decay: As the group spread across Teyvat it became a loose criminal syndicate, self-styling as "returning treasures to connoisseurs." 4. Meaning distortion: "Freedom" devolved into greed; "boundless" shifted from "breaking class barriers" to "ignoring legal boundaries." This corruption mirrors Nod-Krai itself as an "adventurer's paradise" — idealism consumed by utilitarianism in the absence of order.
